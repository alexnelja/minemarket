# Africa Forex Crisis, Barter Systems & the Post-Dollar World

**Research Date:** March 2026

---

## 1. African Countries with Severe Forex Problems

### The Core Problem

Multiple African countries face chronic dollar shortages — demand for hard currency (imports, debt servicing, capital outflows) structurally exceeds supply (exports, tourism, remittances, FDI). The pandemic crushed hard-currency earnings, global inflation spiked import costs, and rising US interest rates strengthened the dollar while increasing debt burdens.

### Country-by-Country Breakdown

#### Nigeria
- Naira at ~N1,383/USD (March 2026), partially stabilized from N1,500 in early 2025
- De facto multiple exchange rate system — black market premium historically up to 77%, now narrowed to ~N10
- CBN relies on high interest rates to attract "hot money" (foreign portfolio investment) — experts warn this is artificial and unsustainable
- Feb 2026: new liquidity controls, tightened monetary policy, expedited forex backlog clearing
- **Paradox:** Africa's largest oil producer imports refined petroleum, creating a dollar drain

#### Egypt
- Egyptian pound lost >66% of value against dollar since early 2022
- **Turning point** (March 2024): floated the pound, increased IMF loan, landed $35B Ras El Hekma UAE deal
- FX reserves recovered to ~$44-45B by early 2026
- Some businesses still face conversion/repatriation difficulties

#### Ethiopia
- **July 29, 2024:** National Bank floated the birr (precondition for $10.7B IMF/World Bank financing)
- Birr devalued ~100% in <2 weeks: ETB 57 → ETB 114/USD
- Late 2024: official ETB 136/USD, parallel ~ETB 160/USD
- Parallel market premium narrowed from 65 birr to 14 birr (Jul-Dec 2024) — liberalization working directionally
- Export revenues hit historic $8B for 2024/25 fiscal year

#### Ghana
- Cedi lost ~19% against dollar in 2024
- Under $3B IMF Extended Credit Facility (approved May 2023), fifth review completed Dec 2025
- **GoldBod initiative** (March 2025): centralized gold purchasing/exporting, generated ~$8B, boosted reserves from 2 weeks to ~4.5 months of import cover
- Inflation: 54% (late 2023) → moderating significantly

#### Zimbabwe
- Launched **ZiG (Zimbabwe Gold)** currency April 2024, claimed backed by $900M in gold + foreign currencies
- Devalued 43% by central bank (Sep 2024), lost ~94% of launch value on unofficial market by Feb 2025
- Official rate stabilized ~25.57 ZiG/USD (Feb 2026), parallel ~36/USD
- Inflation fell to 4.1% (Jan 2026) — first single-digit reading in 2+ decades
- Informal sector broadly refuses ZiG, prefers USD. Government plans ZiG as sole legal tender by 2026

#### Angola
- Kwanza depreciated 44% since early 2023
- Remittances abroad take up to 3 months due to FX shortage
- Reintroduced CEOC tax on foreign currency transfers (Dec 2023)
- Inflation: 28% peak (2024) → 20% (2025)
- July 2025: worst street protests in decades over diesel subsidy removal (22 dead, 1,200 arrested)

#### Mozambique
- April 2025: mandatory forex conversion for exporters raised from 30% to 50%; 100% for petroleum re-exports
- Feb 2025: 63 companies had $373M in pending import payments stuck >6 months
- Dollar crunch hit fuel supplies — Petromoc warned of gas station supply exhaustion
- Aug 2025: net international reserves climbed to 10-year high of $4.04B

#### Kenya (Success Story)
- Largely resolved acute dollar shortage
- Shilling appreciated 17.4% in 2024 (Kshs 157 → 129.3/USD) — first appreciation in 6 years
- Forex reserves: $9.3B (Feb 2025), 4.7 months import cover, up 28.2% YoY

### Common Patterns
1. Export concentration in small number of commodities → vulnerable to price swings
2. Dollar-denominated debt creates structural drain on reserves
3. Import dependence (especially fuel and food) ensures persistent dollar demand
4. Capital flight accelerates during crises as citizens seek hard currency
5. Parallel markets emerge wherever official rates are artificially maintained

## 2. Countries Effectively on Barter Systems / Currency Breakdown

### Zimbabwe — The Archetype
Despite launching ZiG in 2024, the informal sector broadly rejects local currency in favor of USD. Zimbabwe operates as a substantially dollarized economy with local currency used mainly for government payments and small transactions.

### Modern "Barter" — Minerals for Security Deals

A significant and growing form of **state-level barter** across Africa:

- **Russia** trades military assistance (former Wagner Group PMCs) for mining rights across the Sahel (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, CAR)
- **China** offers infrastructure for long-term mineral resource control. Chinese policy banks issued $24.9B in BRI-linked mining loans in H1 2025 alone. Recent acquisitions: Botswana's Khoemacau copper mine, Mali's Goulamina lithium, Tanzania's Ngualla rare earths
- **US** brokered June 2025 DRC-Rwanda peace deal including arrangements for American investors to access "transparent, formalized end-to-end mineral chains"
- Competing infrastructure: US-backed Lobito Railway Corridor vs China's Tanzania-Zambia railway

### Mobile Money as De Facto Currency

Rather than reverting to physical barter, economies have leapfrogged to mobile money:

- **Kenya:** 59% of GDP flows through M-Pesa. 82% of adults use mobile money, ~70% of informal businesses rely on it
- African mobile money market: $951M (2025) → projected $4.3B (2034), 18.32% CAGR
- **COMESA DRPP** launched Oct 2025: lets businesses in member countries trade directly in local currencies

### Stablecoin Dollarization

A new form of informal dollarization spreading rapidly:
- Nigeria: persistent naira volatility pushed households/SMEs into USDT and USDC as working capital and savings
- Sub-Saharan Africa: stablecoin growth >180% YoY
- This is bottom-up dollarization that central banks cannot control

## 3. The Dollar Hegemony Question

### BRICS De-Dollarization Efforts

**The "Unit" — Gold-Backed Settlement Token:**
- Oct 31, 2025: researchers launched pilot for gold-anchored settlement "Unit" inside BRICS+
- Dec 8, 2025: prototype uses 40% gold / 60% BRICS-currency basket, adjusted daily
- Designed as trade settlement mechanism, NOT a replacement for domestic currencies

**Gold Accumulation:**
- BRICS+ central banks added ~800 tonnes of gold in 2025
- Combined reserves >6,000 tonnes (~20-21% of global central bank gold)
- Russia: 2,336t; China: 2,298t (together 74% of bloc's total)

**Local Currency Settlement:**
- BRICS+ nations now settle >85% of mutual trade in local currencies (up from 65% in late 2024)
- Dollar/euro share of intra-BRICS transactions fallen below 35%

**Key Limitation:** Internal divisions remain. India wary of yuan dominance. No single BRICS currency on the horizon — only settlement mechanisms.

### China's Yuan Internationalization in Africa
- Currency swap agreements with South Africa, Morocco, Egypt, Nigeria
- July 2025: China-Egypt agreements to promote yuan in bilateral trade
- CIPS reached 1,683 participants (May 2025), processing >RMB 175.49T ($24.45T) annually
- Africa positioned as testing ground for yuan internationalization
- **But:** yuan still only ~3-4% of global payments

### CBDCs in Africa
- **Nigeria eNaira** (launched 2021): Africa's first retail CBDC, targeting 70% unbanked. Adoption slow.
- **South Africa Project Khokha:** wholesale CBDC pilot, also in cross-border pilots with Australia, Malaysia, Singapore
- >75% of surveyed African countries engaged in CBDC research/pilots
- >25% actively preparing to launch by 2028

### What Would Replace the Dollar?

**Nothing in the near term.** The realistic trajectory is a fragmented, multipolar system:

1. **No single replacement** — yuan lacks capital account openness, euro has structural problems, gold can't function as modern payment medium at scale
2. **Regional settlement systems** — PAPSS for Africa, BRICS Unit for BRICS+ trade, CIPS for China-linked trade
3. **Selective de-dollarization** — specific bilateral corridors (China-Russia, China-Saudi) while dollar retains global reserve status
4. **Digital currencies** — CBDCs and stablecoins may enable more direct currency-to-currency settlement

### Impact on Africa
- Most vulnerable to dollar strength (commodity-export dependence, dollar-denominated debt)
- De-dollarization could benefit if it leads to local-currency trade settlement (eliminates "double conversion" cost)
- **Risk:** swapping dollar dependence for yuan dependence if China becomes dominant alternative
- PAPSS and AfCFTA represent Africa's own path

## 4. African Currency Solutions Being Tried

### PAPSS (Pan-African Payment and Settlement System)
- Launched Jan 13, 2022 by AU and Afreximbank
- As of 2025: 19 countries connected, 150+ commercial banks, 14 payment switches
- **PAPSSCARD** (June 2025): Africa's first continental card scheme, challenging Visa/Mastercard
- **PACM** (July 2025): African Currency Marketplace — direct P2P exchange of African currencies without dollar intermediation
- Target: save $5B annually in transaction costs

### Afreximbank
- Disbursed >$17.5B in trade finance (2024), targeting $40B by 2026
- $10B AfCFTA Adjustment Fund
- Africa faces ~$100B trade finance gap; SME financing gap >$300B

### CFA Franc Zone — Colonial Currency Debate
- 14 sub-Saharan countries pegged to euro via France. 160M+ people, ~$283B combined GDP
- **Reform momentum accelerating:**
  - Burkina Faso, Niger, Mali announced intentions to leave CFA franc
  - Senegal's President Faye made ending CFA franc a campaign theme (elected 2024)
  - ECOWAS plans "eco" currency beginning 2027 (repeatedly delayed)

### Crypto Adoption
- Sub-Saharan Africa: $205B+ in on-chain crypto value (Jul 2024 - Jun 2025), up 52% YoY
- Nigeria ranks 2nd globally on Crypto Adoption Index
- 4 Sub-Saharan countries in 2026 global top 20: Nigeria, Ethiopia, Kenya +1
- Kenya passed VASP Act (Oct 2025)
- Primary use: cross-border remittances, merchant payments, savings dollarization via stablecoins

## 5. Commodity-Backed Currency Potential

### The Proposal
AfDB and KPMG South Africa proposed pooling mineral resources (copper, cobalt, nickel, lithium) into a commodity basket backing a currency or settlement unit.

### Zimbabwe's ZiG — Live Experiment
- Gold backing alone cannot compensate for lack of institutional credibility, fiscal discipline, and public trust
- 94% loss on parallel market within a year despite claimed $900M in gold reserves
- Some stabilization by Feb 2026 — concept may work over time if sustained

### Ghana's GoldBod — The Pragmatic Alternative
- Rather than backing currency with gold, centralized gold purchasing/export to capture forex
- Generated ~$8B since March 2025, dramatically boosting reserves
- **More practical:** use commodities to strengthen reserves rather than directly back currency

### Fundamental Challenges
1. **Price volatility:** commodity prices projected to fall 12% in 2025
2. **Fungibility:** many critical minerals lack standard grading and liquid spot markets
3. **Concentration risk:** 34 African countries depend on ≤3 commodities for >50% of exports
4. **Processing gaps:** most exports are raw ore — value/price-setting sits elsewhere
5. **Governance:** backing only as credible as the institution managing it

### Historical Precedents
- Gold standard collapsed because commodity-linking constrains monetary policy during downturns
- Venezuela's "Petro" (2018): failed completely — lack of trust + sanctions
- Libya's gold dinar proposal: never progressed beyond rhetoric

### What Could Work
- Commodity revenues building robust FX reserves (Ghana GoldBod model)
- PAPSS-style direct local-currency settlement for intra-African trade
- Commodity-linked bonds or trade finance instruments (Afreximbank positioned for this)
- Regional pooling of reserves/commodity revenues to backstop settlement systems

## Key Synthesis

1. **The forex crisis is structural, not cyclical.** Export concentration, import dependence, and dollar-denominated debt. No single reform fixes this.

2. **Formal currency isn't breaking down into barter — it's being replaced by digital alternatives.** Mobile money, stablecoins, and crypto are the real replacements. State-level minerals-for-security deals are the exception.

3. **De-dollarization is real but slow.** BRICS local-currency settlement at 85% for intra-bloc trade, but dollar's global reserve status unchallenged. Africa should focus on reducing dollar dependence for intra-African trade (PAPSS).

4. **PAPSS is the most promising African-led solution.** 19 countries, 150+ banks, PACM currency marketplace. Directly addresses the absurdity of converting naira to dollars to buy Kenyan goods.

5. **Commodity-backed currencies have a poor track record.** Zimbabwe's ZiG proves gold backing ≠ credibility. Ghana's GoldBod (commodities supporting reserves) is more pragmatic.

6. **The real risk is swapping dollar dependence for yuan dependence.** China explicitly using Africa as yuan internationalization testing ground, and minerals-for-infrastructure deals create long-term resource commitments.
